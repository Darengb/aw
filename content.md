## 1. Longevity & Scale (top-level trust signals)

These are the things I’d surface *very* high on the page (hero + just below):

* **“Celebrating 40 years of success”** – the company is currently positioning itself this way on its own site, tied to helping people out of poverty through meaningful employment. ([America Works][1])
* **Nearly 2,000,000 individuals served** – their About page says they’ve helped nearly 2M people increase self-sufficiency via gainful employment, including veterans, SNAP recipients, justice-involved individuals, homeless individuals, non-custodial parents, and people receiving disability. ([America Works][2])
* **Operating since mid-1980s** – leaders in workforce solutions since the 1960s and “helping launch careers since 1984.” ([America Works][3])
* **Corporate history & federal registration** – America Works of New York, Inc. is recorded as founded in 1985, with continuous federal registration since 2003 under NAICS 561311 (Employment Placement Agencies); registration is active as of 2025. ([HigherGov][4])

These are *great* for a top strip like:

> “40 years of experience • Nearly 2 million people served • Active partner to federal, state & local agencies since the 1980s”

---

## 2. Outcomes & Model (what makes them different)

For both jobseekers and government buyers, the performance-based, “work first” story is a differentiator:

* **Blended service model** – work readiness training, vocational training, career placement, career advancement and employment retention, tailored to populations with barriers. ([America Works][2])
* **“Most job seekers working again within four weeks”** – their jobseekers page claims that most clients are back to work within four weeks, with continued support after placement (problem-solving, reminders, extra training for growth). ([America Works][5])
* **Performance-based / outcomes-driven** – external policy write-ups highlight that America Works’ most important selling point is its performance-based fee structure, which gives a strong incentive to get clients off welfare and keep them employed, as opposed to traditional training programs. ([City Journal][6])
* **Long-term cost effectiveness** – think-tank analysis notes that, although they may charge more upfront than some nonprofits, their clients tend to stay off welfare longer, making them cheaper over time. ([Manhattan Institute][7])

This can fuel homepage blocks like:

* “Outcomes first: paid for results, not activity.”
* “Most jobseekers we serve are working again in about a month, with continued retention support.”

(Obviously you’d soften/time-bound that claim depending on legal.)

---

## 3. Services & Support for Jobseekers

To reassure individual jobseekers that this isn’t just a government contractor, but a hands-on support system:

* **Job search & readiness services**

  * Resume writing
  * Interview training (“Interview 101”)
  * Career coaching
  * Access to employment opportunities
  * Retention services and job replacement if needed
  * Career advancement help ([America Works][8])
* **Practical supports** – mention of weekly MetroCard and bonus incentives for some programs, which signals they understand transportation and financial barriers. ([America Works][8])
* **Ongoing support post-hire** – they emphasize staying with clients “every step of the way” during transition into a new job, including reminders, troubleshooting, and additional training depending on career choices. ([America Works][5])
* **Ticket to Work provider** – America Works of New York is listed as a full-service Employment Network under Social Security’s Ticket to Work program, which is a big trust marker for disabled jobseekers. ([Ticket to Work][9])

These details can power a “How we help you” section, plus icons or steps (Prepare → Place → Support → Advance).

---

## 4. Coverage & Footprint (for both audiences)

Government evaluators want to see reach; jobseekers want to know it’s not just one office:

* **Multi-state presence** – office locations listed in: California, District of Columbia, Georgia, Louisiana, Maryland, New Jersey, New York, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Vermont, and Wisconsin. ([America Works][10])
* **Multiple office sites in major cities** – e.g., dedicated centers in the Bronx and Brooklyn described as long-standing employment agencies focused on connecting job seekers with meaningful opportunities and providing job training, resume assistance, and interview prep. ([MapQuest][11])

Great raw line for homepage:

> “Local teams across 11 states and D.C., combining national expertise with neighborhood-level support.”

---

## 5. Populations Served & Equity Story

They clearly specialize in people with barriers to work, which is appealing both to jobseekers and to public agencies:

* **Range of populations** – welfare and SNAP recipients, young adults, military veterans, justice-involved individuals, homeless individuals, non-custodial parents, and persons receiving disability benefits. ([America Works][2])
* **Partner of public assistance systems** – they’re mentioned in multiple welfare-to-work analyses (Manhattan Institute, Reason, etc.) as a private contractor that moves public-assistance recipients into jobs and participates in performance-based TANF/SNAP programs. ([Manhattan Institute][12])

This supports a DEI-/equity-flavored block like:

> “Specialized in helping people with real barriers to work build sustainable careers.”

---

## 6. Government Credibility & Compliance

This is the stuff you want in a “For Government Partners” block or near the footer to reassure procurement folks:

* **Registered federal vendor** – listed as a corporate entity (not tax-exempt) registered for “Federal Contracts and Assistance” with active registration (UEI, CAGE code), primary NAICS 561311. ([HigherGov][4])
* **Woman-owned business** – self-certified woman-owned status appears in federal registration, which can matter in certain contract competitions. ([HigherGov][4])
* **Audit and oversight history** – New York State Office of the State Comptroller audited its welfare-to-work contract back in the early 1990s, indicating a long history of working under formal monitoring and state oversight. ([Office of the State Comptroller][13])
* **Referenced in national policy debate** – founder testimony to the U.S. House Ways and Means Committee on welfare-to-work implementation and compliance requirements. ([Ways and Means][14])
* **Part of performance-based contracts in major cities** – cited in studies of performance-based contracting in NYC and elsewhere as a key welfare-to-work vendor. ([Rockefeller Institute of Government][15])

These can be distilled into bullets like:

* “Decades of experience delivering performance-based contracts for state and local agencies”
* “Active, compliant federal vendor (NAICS 561311, woman-owned corporate entity)”
* “Cited in independent policy evaluations as a leading example of outcomes-driven welfare-to-work contracting.”

---

## 7. Reputation & Social Proof

Some extra seasoning you can consider sprinkling in:

* **Employee ratings trend** – their New York location’s ratings on Indeed have improved over time (from low scores in 2021–2022 to mid-3s / nearly 4.0 by 2023–2025), which could be used internally to support “investing in our people,” even if you don’t quote the numbers on the homepage. ([Indeed][16])
* **Long history in public discourse** – they’ve been written about in outlets like City Journal and Forbes for decades, usually as a case study in welfare-to-work and performance-based models. ([City Journal][6])

On the homepage, this can become a small “As seen in / referenced by” strip (if they’re comfortable citing think-tanks / publications).

## How I’d translate this into homepage building blocks

You didn’t ask for full copy, so I’ll keep this as a checklist you can hand to a writer/designer:

1. **Hero bar**

   * One-line mission (e.g., ending poverty through work). ([America Works][1])
   * Top stats: *40 years experience • ~2,000,000 people helped • 11 states + D.C.* ([America Works][3])

2. **For Jobseekers section**

   * “Most jobseekers working again within four weeks” + list of concrete services + Ticket to Work logo/line. ([America Works][5])

3. **For Government & Partners section**

   * Performance-based model explanation. ([City Journal][6])
   * Federal registration details + woman-owned status. ([HigherGov][4])
   * “Cited in independent policy research and U.S. congressional testimony” with small summary. ([Rockefeller Institute of Government][15])

4. **Impact & Equity section**

   * Populations served (veterans, justice-involved, homeless, etc.). ([America Works][2])
   * One or two outcome-oriented lines (e.g., “Helping people with barriers build lasting careers, not just get short-term jobs”). ([Manhattan Institute][7])

5. **Locations & Contact section**

   * Map or list of states and note of virtual + in-person services. ([America Works][10])
